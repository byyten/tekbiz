Problems, problems, problems

Computers are sophisticated, complex machines and are generally very reliable. Operating systems do alot to remove complexity simplifying choices down to a few options in mostly logical and intuitive ways, but inevitably things can and will go wrong. Some errors can be as simple to fix as restarting the device. However problems can come about through having made a choice or sequence of choices (changes to a configuration) that may have helped solve one problem but have unintended consequences for something else. Or it could be a permanent or intermitent hardware fault or it could be a multitude of other possibilities ...

We can help to solve problems when they arise. And your help is also needed.

There's no single rule to follow in such cases but it's very helpful to know any of error messages, (take a photo of the screen at the point of failures/errors), and to have a clear recollection of what was being used/done at the time. Any details about what appeared on screen and the sequence of events leading upto can prove also useful - each could help reduce the service costs and time to resolution

apps_outage Pre-installed software

Most often manufacturers pre-install software on new computers and there may be a few different types installed. Manufacturers may have small utilities to configure the hardware and so are generally useful and should never be removed. These "applets" may by used to periodically update drivers specific to the hardware, be used to adjust audio settings or screen parameters or update low level firmware, etc, etc.

Manufacturers also often have licensing arrangements with other companies and include 3rd-party products pre-installed on your new device. You might have for instance a video editing suite, an antivirus product, amongst other software and of course Microsoft (on Windows devices) will entice you with the Office suite of products.

However some of this pre-installed software is to provide assistance, sometimes with time-limited trials and subscriptions, typically designed to draw the purchaser into using some paid services or to buy a license. In other words, this software is optional to you and could be removed before being used The potential problems here are accepting defaults, the most convenient option, because it is the easiest, a few clicks away.

Consumer electronics are designed to be convenient and easy, avoiding all the complexity that they embody and automating many, many processes unknown to you. This is human psychology in action. People not paying sufficient attention, not reading the T&Cs (who wants to read contracts), it’s there, it’s the easiest, it’s now, but there are many commercial and strategic decisions taken prior to it all arriving on your table.

All that convenience can come at additional costs to you. Most often it’s just your money, however if it’s free to use, you often are the "product" which means your data and your privacy. Electronic devices for all the benefits they provide and there are many, they have become central to what’s called the "surveillance economy" and that is big tech at work and unfortunately some bad actors are in the mix too The same is true of many web sites, they offer you something for "free", time limited use of their service. with free and paid tiers that provide more functionality with monthly subscriptions. It’s a lucrative business model.

apps Open Source Software

Less well known to many is the world of Open Source Software. This is software that anyone can download and it’s totally free to use. And it can be as good as or even better than the commercial equivalents albeit it’s perhaps not as "slick " as the commercial ones. Your license fee or subscription pays for features you do not use. Open Source is nearly always a very competent software, developed by people who are passionate about that field and would most likely cover all of the functionality you may require. A few examples are:

GIMP – A photo / image manipulation program and a competitor of Adobe Photoshop. GIMP, while an extremely competent software, it is not as sophisticated as Photoshop but then Photoshop is and always has been, a product for professionals.

InkScape – A diagramming / illustration program and a competitor of Adobe Illustrator, Corel Draw or Visio. Much the same can be said as for GIMP

LibreOffice/OpenOffice – Are open source equivalents to Microsoft Office. Microsoft Office is a sprawling suite of applications and packaged in a multitude of ways. The open source options LibreOffice and OpenOffice equally have the majority of applications that are bundled with MS Office and are eminently competent software

Mozilla Firefox/Thunderbird – are a browser and email client that are open source equivalents to Edge & Outlook (Microsoft email client). Browsers and email clients are typically free to download and use, whoever makes them, but Outlook may be a paid service when bundled with Office.

Chrome - Google is the best search engine hands down and Google offers the Chrome browser, but in default settings, Google is siphoning off so much data about you and your activity, it would make your head spin. This is Big Tech, Big Data, Big Money, targeted advertising, 'profiling' and the 'surveillance economy'. Some people are OK with that, some are blissfully ignorant of those facts, others not so keen.

Problems that plague commercial software like bugs and security vulnerabilities are generally lesser in open source software for a couple of reasons. Primarily bad actors tend to target the major products in the marketplace. Secondly, with open source software many people can read the program source code for the product - many eyes identify and eliminate most of the problems and automated software can scan for the typical vulnerabilities found in software. However, for commercial software, the code base is closed source, a proprietary secret, so fewer eyes get to see and work on it. Security scanning and safe coding practices may be laxer or even absent.

The key take aways of this message are: open source software is free to use, for every commercial product, there is nearly always an Open Source alternative and finally commercial software does not necessarily mean better or safer software but it is guaranteed to cost you more to use.

But the real bonus with Open Source Software is you can terminate many of those subscriptions and licenses and buy a few more lattes or whatever floats your marshmellow.

battery_status_good Battery Care

One of the recent technical game changers was the advent of Lithium Ion (li-ion) batteries and a few closely related variants. It made batteries smaller and hold more power. Correspondingly, technology became smaller and more portable. Now almost everything has a battery in it.

As with everything, for all the upsides this brings, there are some downsides.

A characteristic of this battery chemistry, is that they "lose memory", meaning that after repeated charge/discharge cycles (typical usage patterns) they can no longer charge back up to the original 100% and the total percentage charge degrades over time. Two options exist for older batteries that do not have much "autonomy" (can’t hold a decent charge), either replace the battery or replace the entire product. Often it’s the product because of the economics of replacements and/or technical obsolescence. Therefore, it’s better to take care of batteries when new, to make them last longer.

It’s easier to say than to do but the good battery care guide would say:

  • Discharge completely the battery before putting the device on charge again
  • Once fully or near fully charged, disconnect the charger / take it off charging.
  • Switch device off if it’s not to be used and unplug
  • If a device is to be left unused for an extended period, remove the battery (if possible), store in a neutral, dry area

These are the basic rules. There are also some don’t ever dos and some be awares

  • Do not dispose of the battery in the household rubbish – they’re a potential fire hazard and toxic in the environment.
  • Some electronics equipment have built in power management and stop the battery from being over-charged but that depends entirely on the quality/cost of the product but you shouldn’t rely on that.
  • Like everything manufactured, a very large percentage of the products meet all the required safety standards and pass manufacturing line tests however there will always be a percentage, however small that fail and with Li-ion batteries, when they fail, it can be very dangerous. Heat is often a precursor to failure, when a device is left on charge longer than necessary or left in the sun, the battery can swell up in size and pop open or worse ignite in a run-away ignition
  • If the battery suffers physical damage/deformation then it should be replaced. Do not use the battery

The problem with probabilities is that the probability will eventually happen. A probability of one in a million will happen on average once in a million times. You just do want to be the one involved when that small probability happens.

security The Internet (World Wide Web)

The Wonderful World Wide Web is truly a huge treasure trove, full of delight. It’s the largest library and art gallery on the planet, with entertainments everywhere. It’s the biggest department store and supermarket, the biggest storage locker there is, ever. You can even find out what’s happening in real time, connect and socialise, collaborate and work there, not to mention it is the progenitor of the latest and greatest technologies.

But as with everything, while it hosts many absolutely brilliant, beautiful, wonderful and practical things (upsides), it also has some real bummers (downsides). Queue in please viruses, trojans, worms, scams, identity and financial theft, hackers, bigotry, disinformation, keyboard-warriors, anti-social behaviour, crime and some truly dark corners of total depravity and the worst of human kind.

This is not an exhaustive or even a very long list of potential dangers and nasties out there in the world wide web. Within each group of threats, there’s a multitude of techniques and levels of danger. The Internet can be a dangerous place for the unsuspecting and unwary.

When you meet someone face to face in society, typically it’s in a trusting manner, society would not work very well if this were not the case. However on-line (the Internet) the dynamic has changed completely to the opposite. What outwardly and superficially looks decent, innocent and honest could actually be quite or even very deceptive.

Humans are not fast enough and cannot see what happens underneath the slick surface presentation (not without the right tools anyway) – as always it depends – who, what, where, when. Operating on the Internet and staying away from trouble is not that difficult but it requires vigilance, attention, a bit of discipline and a bit of scepticism (unfortunately).
